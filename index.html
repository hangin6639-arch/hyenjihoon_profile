CONTEC — Diagnosing a Brand Communication Gap with NLP

Team project (강우주 · 차서진 · 한보민 · 현지훈) · My focus: the NLP measurement pipeline
Sentence-BERT · TF-IDF · co-occurrence networks · 2026
Code: github.com/hangin6639-arch/brand_alignment_project

CONTEC is a KARI spin-off operating in the space downstream: it builds small-satellite platforms, runs its own global ground-station network, and sells AI-analyzed satellite data — a rare end-to-end vertical chain. Strong fundamentals, real policy tailwinds, and yet the brand barely registers in the market. So we asked one blunt question: does the market actually understand what CONTEC is? I built an NLP pipeline to measure how the company's identity gets distorted as it travels from internal documents → PR → news coverage.

Background — the tailwinds are real

The global space economy keeps growing, and the downstream segment (data & services, exactly CONTEC's turf) is the fastest-expanding slice of the value chain.

Policy points the same way: in the KASA era, CONTEC is repeatedly framed as a key downstream partner and policy beneficiary.

The tailwinds are there. The open question is whether that value reaches the market intact.

So how is CONTEC's value actually transmitted to the market?

Method — brand communication as three layers

I modeled the journey of meaning as three layers, each with its own text corpus:

  • Layer 1 — Identity: what the company is — business reports / IR filings (DART).
  • Layer 2 — PR: what the company says — homepage newsroom (45 posts crawled).
  • Layer 3 — Market: what the market repeats — 376 Naver news articles (2023–2026).

Every sentence is embedded with Sentence-BERT — a bi-encoder that produces sentence-level embeddings, so two whole sentences can be compared directly rather than token-by-token.

For each layer I took the centroid of its sentence vectors and projected it onto four “brand anchor” axes (ground-station/GSaaS, space-data pipeline, hardware ecosystem, policy/theme) via cosine similarity. Tracking how that profile shifts from layer to layer gives a concrete semantic-distortion metric — the distance between layer centroids.

Findings — three problems

Problem 1 — Meaning is diluted and distorted in transit. Across the four anchors, the Identity, PR, and Market profiles don't line up. PR over-amplifies some axes, while the market collapses the brand onto “policy / theme.”

After filtering noise, the gap sharpens: what the company is (green) and what the market hears (red) diverge most on the technical axes — data pipeline and hardware — the very things that make CONTEC distinctive.

Problem 2 — A severe brand-awareness problem. I built a co-occurrence knowledge graph from the news corpus. Even though we crawled with “CONTEC” as the seed keyword, CONTEC shows up as a faint background node rather than an independent hub — the market reads it as part of the industry backdrop, not a distinct brand.

Problem 3 — No emotional brand communication. A TF-IDF read of CONTEC coverage skews almost entirely to System-2 (rational / logical) vocabulary — “industry,” “satellite,” “technology” — with little System-1 (emotional / intuitive) language. Purchase decisions lean on intuition; CONTEC's language barely engages it.

What I took away

  • Building the pipeline forced me to make “brand perception” measurable — defining anchors, centroids, and a distortion metric instead of eyeballing word clouds.
  • The honest lesson: a strong vertical-chain company can still lose its narrative when identity, PR, and market language drift apart.
  • On the team, this measurement layer was my main contribution; the marketing strategy (STP / 4P) was built on top of these diagnoses.
